Transforming Skewed Continuous Features¶

A dataset may sometimes contain at least one feature whose values tend to lie near a single number, but will also have a non-trivial number of vastly larger or smaller values than that single number. Algorithms can be sensitive to such distributions of values and can underperform if the range is not properly normalized. With the census dataset two features fit this description: 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss'.

Run the code cell below to plot a histogram of these two features. Note the range of the values present and how they are distributed.

For highly-skewed feature distributions such as 'capital-gain' and 'capital-loss', it is common practice to apply a logarithmic transformation on the data so that the very large and very small values do not negatively affect the performance of a learning algorithm. Using a logarithmic transformation significantly reduces the range of values caused by outliers. Care must be taken when applying this transformation however: The logarithm of 0 is undefined, so we must translate the values by a small amount above 0 to apply the the logarithm successfully.

Metrics and the Naive Predictor¶

CharityML, equipped with their research, knows individuals that make more than \$50,000 are most likely to donate to their charity. Because of this, *CharityML* is particularly interested in predicting who makes more than \$50,000 accurately. It would seem that using accuracy as a metric for evaluating a particular model's performace would be appropriate. Additionally, identifying someone that does not make more than \$50,000 as someone who does would be detrimental to *CharityML*, since they are looking to find individuals willing to donate. Therefore, a model's ability to precisely predict those that make more than \$50,000 is more important than the model's ability to recall those individuals. We can use F-beta score as a metric that considers both precision and recall:

$$ F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{precision \cdot recall}{\left( \beta^2 \cdot precision \right) + recall} $$

In particular, when $\beta = 0.5$, more emphasis is placed on precision. This is called the F$_{0.5}$ score (or F-score for simplicity).

Looking at the distribution of classes (those who make at most \$50,000, and those who make more), it's clear most individuals do not make more than \$50,000. This can greatly affect accuracy, since we could simply say "this person does not make more than \$50,000" and generally be right, without ever looking at the data! Making such a statement would be called naive, since we have not considered any information to substantiate the claim. It is always important to consider the naive prediction for your data, to help establish a benchmark for whether a model is performing well. That been said, using that prediction would be pointless: If we predicted all people made less than \$50,000, CharityML would identify no one as donors.

Gaussian Naive Bayes

Gaussian Naive Bayes is a general purpose classification algorithm that utilises Bayes theorem and a strong assumption of feature independence to classify datapoints. It is used in a wide array of applications often used as a benchmark for classification performance, as will be its use here. One such example is using a NB classifier to predict the correct category for new Google Play store apps (Olabenjo, 2016). One of the major advantages of NB is that it handles a large number of features extremely well and its also a relatively easy algorithm to understand and implement. This makes it great for benchmarking classification performance and for use when we have a classification problem with a large number of independent features. On the other hand the model can struggle in situations where this assumption of feature independence is violated, as the case may be with financial or medical data. Personally I think this model will prove valuable as a benchmark for the performance of the other algorithms to be tested, despite the fact that the assumption of independence is likely violated by some of the above features. Gaussian NB should be able to quickly and easily handle the above data and give us a starting point for further analysis.

Gradient Boosting

Gradient Boosting is an example of an ensemble learning algorithm that combines multiple weak learners in an attempt to create a strong learner. GB has proved to be a very successful technique for a wide variety of problems including and especially for Kaggle competitions where it has been responsible for a number of competition wins (Gorman, 2017). Aside from great performance, GB like other boosting methods tends to be quite robust to overfitting. It's also learner agnostic, which means you can use any ML algorithm with it. It's also quite robust to the curse of dimensionality (for some types of underlying weak learners) because each weak learner can choose which features to focus on. There are however some problems associated with GB and boosting in general which include being difficult to tune, hard to interpret and taking a long time to train. Also boosting in general will overfit if the underlying models are allowed to overfit. Personally I'm quite keen to try out GB on the dataset because of its success across a wide variety of domains and datasets at Kaggle and also because its an ensemble method, unlike the others I will be testing. I wouldn't be surprised if GB proves to be the most successful classifier out of the box out of the three that I will be testing.

K-Nearest Neighbors

K-nearest neighbors is a non-parametric (instance based) method used for classification and regression tasks. It is widely used in cases where similarity can be defined and is deemed to be useful for the purpose of classification and also in situations when we don't want to make assumptions regarding the distribution of our data. In a paper titled 'Classification of Heart Disease using K-Nearest Neighbor and Genetic Algorithm', (Akhil jabbar, Deekshatulu and Chandra, 2013) the authors attempt to use and improve apon the k-nearest neighbors algorithm for the purpose of diagnosing heart disease. K-nearest neighbors has the advantage of being non-parametric which means it doesn't need to make assumptions about the underlying distribution of features, which can be extremely useful when making such an assumption would bias our results or when we don't know the underlying distribution. Another benefit is that it requires little to no time for training/retraining, since it uses the raw data to make classifications. KNN is also simple to explain and understand. KNN has some problems however that include long query times (as it is required to iterate over the entire dataset) and having difficulty ignoring irrelevant features. These issues can make KNN inappropriate for situations where repeated querying of the model is required and in situations where the number of features relative to the number of datapoints is high. KNN is also not suitable for problems where a distance metric cannot be appropriately defined or where similarity is not useful for classification. I think this model is a good choice for the data as I believe similarity based on the above features will prove effective for classification. We also have a sufficient number of observations when compared to the number of features, whilst not having too many that the run time of the model becomes an issue.
